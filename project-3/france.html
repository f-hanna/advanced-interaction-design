French Classicism

Mid 1600s - Early 1700s

Much like the rest of Europe, French Classicism was deeply influenced by the principles of order and reason derived from ancient Greek and Roman art.

However, the French Classicism period was unique compared to the rest of Europe because the arts were heavily utilized to glorify King Louis XIV, the Sun King. Under his rule, France saw the centralization of power, the establishment of absolute monarchy, and the consolidation of French cultural influence in Europe.

Another notable aspect of this time frame was the French Salons, a social gathering where a guests discussed not only art, but also literature, philosophy, music, and politics. The democratization of ideas set the roots for the Age of Enlightment, and would set up France as an intellectual powerhouse in Europe.

The Swing

Jean-Honoré Fragonard

Rococo

Early 1700s - Mid 1700s

The Rococo period emerged as a reponse to the Classicism era, known for its ornate and playful aesthetic compared to Classicism's focus on reason, mirroring France's frivolous spending by the aristocracy.

This era revolved around the aristocracy, who moved from Versailles to Paris, embracing leisure and indulging in romantic intrigues, creating a culture of luxury and excess. This contrasted sharply with the majority of French society, as the aristocracy, a small fraction of the population, owned 90% the country's wealth.

While the aristocracy controlled and spent a majority of the country's finances, the common people were effectvely starving, especially as France's economy could no longer handle th aristocrat's luxurious lifestyle. This anger and frustration towards aristocracy would culminate in the French Revolution, which would effectly cause Rococo to heavly decline.

Neoclassicism

Mid 1700s - Early 1800s

Neoclassicism emerged as a reaction to the frivolity and excessive ornament of the baroque and rococo styles, invoking themes of harmony, rationality, stoicism, universality, and idealism based off the ancient Romans and Greeks.

The age of Enlightenment was one of the biggest influences of not only France, but also Europe. The philosophical ideas of individual liberty, constitutional government, and seperation of church and state. These ideas permeated into Neoclassicism themes.

This era also served as the backdrop to one of France's most politically tumultuous era: the bloody French Revolution, the Napoleon's militarisic empire, the industrialization of France. Feelings such as nationalism evoked from these events were translated into Neoclassicism paintings.

Liberty Leading the People

Eugéne Delacroix

Romanticism

Late 1700s - Mid 1800s

Romanticists embraced subjectivity, imagination, and appreciation of nature. Rejecting rationality and stoicism, Romanticism is best defined by its focus on emotion and individualism as well as the glorification of the past and nature.

During the Industrial Revolution, Romanicists grew disillusioned with the rapid progress, especially as industrialization exposed the dehumanizing effects of mechanization, urbanization, and capitalist exploitation. As such, artists longed for a more harmonious and natural way of life.

The movement was arguably a reaction against Enlightenment, challenging it's emphasis on reason, rationality, and order. Romanticism instead celebrated emotion, individualism, imagination, and the sublime.

Impression, Sunrise

Claude Monet

Impressionism

Late 1800s

Impressionism was focused on capturing contemporary life and the ephemeral effects of light and color, viewing these as a crucial element of human perception and experience.

Similar to Romanticists, Impressionist art also responded to rapid industrialization. Rather than rejecting urbanization, artists focused on the modern contemporary life, capturing fleeting moments in the bustling city, reflecting the dynamism and energy of urban existence. Inventions such as the camera inspired artists: artists wanted to emulate the camera's ability to capture spontaneous moments and fleeting effects of light.

Impressionism also reflected the demographic shifts of the time. With industrialization emerged a new economic class: the middle class. This economic expansion created new consumer markets and cultural tastes. Leisure acitivities that previously was rstricted to the aristocrats could now be enjoyed by more people.
